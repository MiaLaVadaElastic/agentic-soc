Definition

The agentic security operations platform, defined

Definition — Agentic Security Operations Platform

An agentic security operations platform is a unified system in which autonomous AI agents handle the full security lifecycle — from data ingestion and alert correlation through threat investigation and response execution — while human analysts retain final judgment, approval authority, and strategic direction. Autonomous agents handle the full lifecycle from ingestion through response. Your analysts handle judgment, verification, and approval. It is distinguished from legacy SIEM, XDR, and SOAR tools by its capacity for adaptive reasoning rather than rigid, pre-scripted playbooks.

Not this

A fully autonomous SOC

The agentic security operations platform is not a fully autonomous SOC. The human is not removed from the loop — they are moved to the top of it. The platform investigates, correlates, and builds the response plan. The analyst reads it, judges it, and approves it.

Not this

A SOAR with an AI layer

Legacy SOAR tools execute deterministic playbooks. Agentic platforms reason through novel situations dynamically — they are architecturally different, not incrementally better.

Not this

A next-generation SIEM

A SIEM ingests and correlates. An agentic platform ingests, reasons, investigates, builds a case, proposes a response, and executes it — in one unified system.

Core capabilities

What an agentic platform requires

Three integrated capability areas define an agentic security operations platform. These are not separate products — they are a single, unified system. Every boundary between them is a delay the adversary exploits.

01
Ingestion at scale
Perceive everything, miss nothing

An agentic platform can only reason over data it can see. Coverage gaps — whether forced by per-device pricing or rigid data pipelines — become attack surface. AI-driven threats are engineered to find unmonitored assets.

02
Reason at machine speed
AI grounded in your data, transparent by design

The reasoning layer is where agentic platforms differ most from legacy tools. AI decisions must be grounded in your actual security data — not a connector abstraction — and transparent enough that analysts can validate every conclusion.

03
Prevention and response
From detection to containment at machine speed

Prevention is the fastest possible response. But when an active incident requires containment, the response must execute faster than a human queue can process. Human judgment stays at the center — every action approved before it executes.

Evaluation guide

Questions to ask before you commit to a platform

Not every platform claiming the agentic label was built for it. These questions help security leaders separate architectural reality from marketing positioning.

1

Can the platform ingest all data sources without pricing-forced coverage gaps?

Per-device or per-source pricing that requires you to choose which assets to monitor is incompatible with an agentic model. AI-driven attacks probe everything. If your coverage has gaps, your defense does too.

Critical
2

Does the platform reason adaptively, or execute pre-scripted playbooks?

Deterministic automation breaks when attackers deviate from expected patterns. An agentic platform uses skills invoked dynamically by an agent reasoning through each incident — including attacks it has never encountered before.

Critical
3

Can analysts see and validate every AI decision?

Transparent AI — visible prompts, auditable reasoning, verifiable outputs — is not optional. If your AI cannot show its work, your analysts cannot trust its conclusions. Unauditable AI decisions are an emerging compliance liability.

Critical
4

Is automation native to the platform, or a separate integration layer?

A standalone SOAR creates an integration point that fails during active incidents — exactly when response speed matters most. Native automation in the same system as detection eliminates this structural failure mode.

Important
5

Can historical data be queried in real time during an active incident?

Rehydration delays that make historical logs unavailable for hours are a structural vulnerability during active campaigns. An agentic platform requires years of telemetry accessible at query time with no penalty for looking back.

Important
6

Does the platform support model sovereignty and deployment flexibility?

Regulated organizations — particularly government and financial services — require the ability to choose their AI model, including fully disconnected on-premises models. Vendor lock-in to a single LLM is incompatible with data residency and sovereignty requirements.

Strategic
7

Is the platform open and auditable by design?

Open detection rules, open schemas, and open agentic frameworks are the foundation for trust and community validation. A platform that locks its logic in proprietary formats cannot be independently verified or adapted to your specific environment.

A SOAR executes deterministic playbooks: if condition A, then action B. This works when attack patterns match expected scenarios but breaks when adversaries deviate — which AI-powered attacks increasingly do. An agentic platform reasons through novel situations, invoking skills dynamically to reach a conclusion. A skill is a discrete, executable capability — like running a query, looking up a hash, or isolating a host — that an agent selects based on the specific situation. The difference is between a fixed decision tree and an adaptive reasoning system.
Human on the loop describes the agentic SOC model: AI handles investigation, correlation, and response planning autonomously, but a human analyst reviews the complete case and approves every significant response before it executes. The analyst is not reviewing raw alerts — they are reviewing a fully assembled case with AI-generated reasoning they can validate, challenge, or override. This is distinct from "human in the loop," where an analyst must act on each individual alert or escalation.
